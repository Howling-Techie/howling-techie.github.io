Shark Facts

  • Sharks have been around for about 450 million years, making them older than dinosaurs.
  • Great white sharks can go up to three months between meals.
  • Hammerhead sharks have specialized sensors in their heads to detect the electrical signals of their prey.
  • Not all sharks are fearsome predators; some, like the whale shark, are filter feeders.
  • Sharks play a crucial role in maintaining the health of ocean ecosystems by controlling prey populations.

Shark Information

Whale Shark

The gentle giants

The whale shark is not just the largest species of shark, but the largest species of fish! It is a slow-moving filter-feeding carpet shark, meaning it mainly just eats small creatures like plankton. Despite its enormous size, it is not considered a threat to humans, and in fact are considered the gentle giants of the sea.

Whitetip Reef Shark

Cuddle Puddle

Whitetip reef sharks live communally in small home ranges anywhere from 0.2 to 1.8 miles (0.3 to 3 km). In caves, whitetip reef sharks pile on top of each other like logs, lying motionless for hours because this species does not need to move to breathe.

Bamboo Shark

Small and stealthy

Unlike many other shark species, bamboo sharks have the unique ability to pump water over their gills even while resting on the ocean floor. This adaptation allows them to thrive in areas with lower oxygen levels, such as coral reefs and sandy bottoms. Bamboo sharks are nocturnal creatures, primarily active during the night as they search for prey like small fish, crustaceans, and mollusks.

Epaulette shark

Hunter of land and sea

One of the most striking features of the Epaulette Shark is its ability to "walk" on its pectoral and pelvic fins, using a distinctive motion that resembles crawling. This unusual form of locomotion enables the shark to navigate through tight spaces and hunt for prey in areas that other sharks might find inaccessible.
